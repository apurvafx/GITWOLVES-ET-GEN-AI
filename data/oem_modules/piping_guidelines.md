# Piping Guidelines

## Purpose
Defines suction and discharge piping design, support, and nozzle load rules for PUMP-101A to prevent misalignment, hot bearings, worn couplings, vibration, and casing failure.

## Engineering Information
Correct suction and discharge piping design and support prevents misalignment, hot bearings, worn couplings, vibration, and possible casing failure on PUMP-101A.

### General Rules
- Never use the pump as a support for piping.
- Never draw piping into place by applying force to the pump flange connections.
- Do not mount expansion joints so their internal-pressure-induced force acts on the pump flange.
- Flush all piping and fittings before use.
- Arrange piping for hazardous liquids to allow pump flushing before removal.
- Remove protective covers from pipe connections before connecting any pipework.

### Suction Piping (Non-Self-Priming Models)
- Inlet pipe should be one or two sizes larger than the pump inlet bore; use large-radius bends.
- On suction lift, incline piping up toward the pump inlet with eccentric reducers to prevent air locks.
- On positive suction, maintain a constant fall toward the pump.
- Provide a minimum of two pipe diameters of straight section between the elbow and inlet flange (5–10 diameters if NPSH margin is small).
- Inlet strainers should have a net free area of at least three times the inlet pipe area.
- Never throttle on the suction side; never place a valve directly on the pump inlet nozzle.
- Small non-abrasive solids under 0.3 mm may be handled (max 2.5% by volume) — solids must be non-magnetic, non-fibrous, non-scaling, and non-coagulating.

### Suction Piping (Self-Priming Models)
- Keep the inlet pipe as short as possible, airtight, and minimum volume for fast priming.
- Inlet pipe should be no larger than the pump inlet bore; target suction velocity 3–5 m/sec.
- Piping should slope down toward the pump casing suction flange.
- Allow a minimum of two pipe diameters of straight section between elbow and inlet flange.

### Discharge Piping
- Fit a non-return valve to protect against reverse rotation on shutdown (non-self-priming models).
- Self-priming models: pipework expanders should have a maximum divergence angle of 9 degrees; fit a vent/bleed pipe back to the sump if a non-return valve is used; fit a regulating valve unless flow is controlled by system design.
- Typical maximum velocities: 2 m/s suction, 3 m/s discharge (non-self-priming); 3 m/s discharge (self-priming).

### Allowable Nozzle Loads
- The pump complies with ISO 5199 shaft deflection limits, presented in ISO 5199/ISO 13709 (API 610) format.
- Individual forces/moments up to twice the ISO 13709 Table 4 values may be permitted only under Annex F conditions.
- Loads must be multiplied by casing-material and liquid-temperature correction factors (values differ for nickel, austenitic DCI/Alloy 20/titanium, and other CPX materials).
- Any values exceeding the tabulated limits must be referred to the OEM for approval.

## Procedure

### Final Checks
Check tightness of all suction and discharge pipework bolts, and all foundation bolts, before commissioning.

## Inspection Points
- Straight pipe run length between elbow and inlet flange (minimum 2 pipe diameters, 5–10 if NPSH margin is small).
- Inlet strainer net free area (minimum 3× inlet pipe area).
- Suction/discharge velocities against typical maximum limits.
- Nozzle loads against ISO 13709 Table 4 (with correction factors applied).
- Pipework bolt and foundation bolt tightness before commissioning.

## Warnings
- Never use the pump as a support for piping.
- Never draw piping into place by applying force to the pump flange connections — this induces pipe strain and can cause casing failure.
- Never throttle on the suction side, and never place a valve directly on the pump inlet nozzle.
- Do not mount expansion joints so their internal-pressure-induced force acts on the pump flange.
- Any nozzle load values exceeding the tabulated limits must be referred to the OEM for approval before installation.

## Best Practices
- Flush all piping and fittings before use, and arrange piping for hazardous liquids to allow pump flushing before removal.
- Use eccentric reducers on suction lift installations to prevent air locks.
- Apply casing-material and liquid-temperature correction factors when evaluating nozzle loads rather than using the base ISO 13709 Table 4 values directly.
- Recheck pipework and foundation bolt tightness immediately before commissioning.

### FAQ
Q: Why should a valve never be placed directly on the pump inlet nozzle?
A: Throttling on the suction side can starve the pump of NPSH and induce cavitation; a valve directly on the inlet nozzle also risks uneven flow into the impeller eye.

Q: What is the minimum straight pipe run required between an elbow and the inlet flange?
A: Two pipe diameters minimum, extended to 5–10 diameters if the NPSH margin is small.

Q: What standard governs allowable nozzle loads for PUMP-101A?
A: ISO 5199, presented in ISO 13709 (API 610) Table 4 format, with correction factors applied for casing material and liquid temperature.

Q: What size range of solids can non-self-priming PUMP-101A handle in the pumped liquid?
A: Small non-abrasive solids under 0.3 mm, up to 2.5% by volume, provided they are non-magnetic, non-fibrous, non-scaling, and non-coagulating.
